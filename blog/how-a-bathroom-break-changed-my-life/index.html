How a Bathroom Break Changed My Life

Before I ever wrote a line of code I was a security guard, a temp contractor, and a QA tester for virtual guns. This is how a bathroom break at a military simulation company changed everything.

I've had a lot of jobs that had nothing to do with tech.

And I mean a LOT.

We're talking security guard. Temp agency contracts doing whatever they needed done. Jobs where they'd let you go after two weeks with no real explanation and you'd just have to move on to the next thing.

It was hard. I won't pretend it wasn't. But it got me here.

The Jobs Nobody Talks About

Nobody talks about the in-between years. The ones between "I want to be in tech" and "I actually work in tech."

I was out here bouncing between temp agencies, taking whatever came up. One of those gigs put me in a data center securing server racks. Y'all, the room was freezing. I'm talking cold cold. I was working nights, doing patrol, making sure nobody was getting into those racks. I didn't even fully understand at the time that what I was protecting was people's actual data. I just knew it was important enough that if they caught you sleeping on the job you were fired on the spot.

But something happened at that data center that I never forgot.

The Data Center Guy

There was this guy who'd come in late at night with his laptop, do whatever he needed to do with the servers, and leave. One night I finally asked him what he did.

He told me I was young, I worked in a data center, and if I picked up anything in IT I'd be set for life. He even said look around, you're already here, use that to your advantage.

That was it. That was the whole conversation. But it hit different.

I started looking into tech from that night on. I didn't know where I fit yet. Cybersecurity, networking, something. But I knew I wanted in. That seed was planted and it wasn't going anywhere.

How I Ended Up at a Military Simulation Company

Fast forward through more work. Help desk. The patent office doing overnight shifts migrating Windows machines. Eventually I landed at Conflict Kinetics.

If you don't know them, they build synthetic marksmanship training systems for the military. Think virtual weapons simulation. If you shoot something in the head, it should register and go down. My job was making sure it did. I was literally testing virtual guns.

Here's the wild part. I was doing quality assurance without even knowing that's what it was called. I didn't know the title. I didn't know the process had a name. I was just doing it.

I was still dead set on cybersecurity at that point too. I'd gotten my CompTIA A+ through SLAM Technology, was working toward my Network+, had my eyes on the SOC. That was the plan. Stick to the plan.

Then one day I walked past a coworker's desk on the way to the bathroom.

The Bathroom Break

His screen stopped me cold.

VS Code. All those colors. The syntax highlighting. The structure of it. I know people say love at first sight only happens in the movies. I used to think that too. But standing there looking at that screen, that was it for me. That was genuinely the moment.

He was doing Unreal Engine development and I had zero idea what any of it meant. But I needed to know. Immediately.

I went back and asked him everything. What are you building? How does your work connect to what I do? How do I even start learning this? He was patient with me. I was not patient at all. I bombarded that man with questions for the next month straight. He still has me on LinkedIn to this day which honestly says a lot about his character.

That moment didn't just change what I wanted to do. It changed how I thought about learning. I stopped following someone else's plan and started chasing the thing that actually made me curious.

What Happened Next

I signed up for a coding bootcamp. I started building things. I struggled, a lot, but I kept going.

That curiosity I felt standing at that desk? It never went away. It just kept finding new directions. Front end development. QA. Content creation. Building for brands. Hosting a podcast. And now developer relations.

Every step made sense looking back. Even the freezing cold data center. Even the contracts that ended with no warning. Even the bathroom break.

If you're in the in-between years right now, just keep going. The moment that changes everything usually doesn't announce itself.
