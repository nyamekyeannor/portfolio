Why I’m Pivoting to DevRel (And Why It Actually Makes Sense)

June 12, 2026 · 5 min read

On this page

I Didn’t Know What DevRel Was I Was Already Doing It The Comment That Made It Click What Scares Me About This Pivot Why I’m Doing It Anyway

I can’t tell you the exact day I first heard the words developer relations.

But I can tell you that somewhere between bootcamp, building AnnorCode, shooting brand partnership videos, and hosting a podcast, I was already doing the job without knowing it had a name.

I Didn’t Know What DevRel Was

For a long time my goal was to become a React developer. That was the plan. Learn the framework, get the job, grow from there.

But I kept seeing people online who were doing something different. They were building things and talking about what they built. They were making videos, writing blog posts, showing up at conferences. They were connecting with developers in a way that felt less like work and more like just being themselves in public.

One person I watched for years was already doing DevRel before I even knew what to call it. He was streaming on Twitch, running a blog, building in public. And I remember thinking, I want to do something like that. I just didn’t know what that something was yet.

I Was Already Doing It

Here’s the thing nobody told me about DevRel. You might already be doing it.

When companies started reaching out to AnnorCode for brand partnerships, the ask was always the same. Learn the product. Make content about it. Help real people understand what it does and why it matters.

That’s it. That’s the job.

I’d take a SaaS tool, spend time actually using it, figure out what was interesting about it, and then get in front of a camera and talk about it in a way that felt real. Not salesy. Not scripted in a way that felt fake. Just honest.

With Typesense I built demo apps to show what the search tool could actually do. With Leveo I started a video series showing job seekers how to use an AI tool that could genuinely change their search. And the whole time I was doing it I kept thinking this feels different from everything else I’ve done in tech. This feels right.

I found myself coming alive making this content. Scripting the videos. Shooting them. Editing them. Learning the product deeply enough to explain it simply. Giving something back to the tech community at the same time. It didn’t feel like work. It felt like one of my gifts finally finding a place to land.

The Comment That Made It Click

A few weeks ago a friend of mine who works in DevRel left a comment on one of my videos. He said I should seriously consider going into DevRel and the AI space.

I texted him immediately. Not because I needed someone to tell me I could do it. But because when someone you respect who is already doing the thing sees it in you too, something shifts.

We talked. He said he’d been watching my journey, watching me grow, and what I was already doing under AnnorCode was developer advocacy. I just needed to build a portfolio around it and show companies what I’d already been doing.

What Scares Me About This Pivot

I had a coffee chat recently with a friend who’s also in DevRel. I told her I was scared about making this move.

She asked me why.

I couldn’t answer her at first. And then somewhere near the end of the call it hit me and I told her the truth. I’m scared because this role is right within my wheelhouse. This is something I could actually do. Like right now. And when something feels that close and that real it’s terrifying in a way that nothing far away ever is.

It’s not imposter syndrome exactly. It’s more like standing at a door you’ve been walking toward for years and realizing it was never locked.

Why I’m Doing It Anyway

A lot of people know how to code. Not many people know how to teach it. Not many people know how to take something technical and make it feel accessible without dumbing it down. Not many people can get in front of a camera and hold someone’s attention.

That combination is rare. And I’ve been told my whole life that communication is one of my strongest gifts. The content I make, the podcast I host, the partnerships I’ve built under AnnorCode, all of it points to the same thing.

DevRel is where the technical side and the human side come together. Where you get to build things and then explain them. Where you get to learn in public and bring people along with you. Where being a people person is not just a personality trait but part of the actual job description.

That’s where I’m going.

And honestly? I think I’ve been heading there for a while.
